The plan this past summer had been to stay put and start long-overdue renovations on their Oakland hills house. Then came the calls for help, and Ganymede — along with his two partners, Cullen Frandsen and Warren Page — couldn’t say no.

In July, Ganymede, who is 60 and goes by a single name, traveled to North Carolina, helping his father and stepmother with work around their home, cleaning gutters and clearing out bushes and clutter.

It was hours of sweaty manual labor, but Ganymede — HIV-positive for 31 years and at times debilitated by his infection — reveled in feeling so full of energy, and in the opportunity to help others like he’s been helped before.

Ganymede (left), a long-term AIDS survivor, sits with his partners, Cullen Frandsen and Warren Page, at their home in Oakland. Erin Brethauer, Special to The Chronicle

Later in the summer he went to Utah, to visit Cullen’s new granddaughter and help his daughter recover from childbirth. Cullen also had a wedding to attend, and Warren a family reunion.

And Ganymede went to a funeral for another long-term AIDS survivor. He lost dozens of friends in the 1980s and 1990s, reaching a point where his grief became overwhelming. Like many men of his generation, he was emotionally scarred.

This memorial, at least, was for someone who’d lived a full life. “For such a sad event,” Ganymede said, “it was very uplifting.”

“Last Men Standing” made Ganymede and his partners acutely aware of how much the community of survivors is struggling. They’ve gained new appreciation of their fortune — that they have each other and their home, that they are healthy enough to travel and help others.

“You’ve got right now, and you’ve got to make the most of it,” Ganymede said one afternoon, as the three men lounged on their patio sipping lattes Cullen had made.

“Facing mortality opens these pathways to humanity,” Ganymede said, suddenly turning philosophical. “I’ve been blessed to take advantage of that awareness.”

The house, he said, can wait.
